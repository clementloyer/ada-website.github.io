It is often believed that the second movie is always worse than the first one. However, human memory tends to be influenced by nostalgia, and it remains uncertain whether this classic dinner table debate around movie franchises stands on tangible evidence. In this project, we aim to settle this debate by analyzing the movie data from the CMU Movie Summary Corpus supplemented by the TMDB dataset. The additional data set contains an additional category which reveals if a movie is part of a franchise (or not); is used to group same-franchise movies as collections.

In the premices of this data analysis, multiple questions arose.

"Is it worth watching the next movies as the sequel continues?"

“Are franchised movies more successful than non-franchised movies?”.

To answer these questions, we first have to look at what we’re working with !


The orginal CMU dataset contains 42,306 movies, and each movie has various data (e.g., title, year, genre) as well as a summary. This makes a lot of data to handle! Among all these movies, 5,136 were identified as part of a franchise, which still leaves plenty of data to analyse.

{% include graphs/franchise_pie.html %}

But, what is a [franchise]?

Have you heard of Star Wars? Harry Potter? Then you know what a franchise is! They are basically what we often call Sagas. From a data analysis point of view, we consider as movie franchise any sequel of at least two movies, whose stories are connected, or that share similarities in terms of characters, plot, and/or setting. That way, both the Harry Potter series or the multiple remakes of Alice in Wonderland are considered franchises.

Over the last century, there have been lots and lots of movies produced as part of franchises over the world. Some franchises existed for short periods of time with only a few movies, while others continued through decades!
